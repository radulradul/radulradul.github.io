01

Why scale-up is its own discipline

A composition that performs beautifully as a 2-gram button in an inert-atmosphere arc melter has to solve an entirely different set of problems before it becomes a heat exchanger plate or a motor magnet: it has to be producible in bulk, joinable, machinable, and durable under thermal and mechanical cycling.

That gap — between a promising lab result and a component an engineer can actually specify — is where most of my applied work sits. It means treating processing route, not just composition, as a design variable: the same alloy can behave very differently depending on whether it's cast, milled, sintered, or additively built, and picking the right route is often what determines whether a material ever leaves the lab.

The periodic table gives you the composition. The process gives you the part.

Selected outcomes

Lab-developed materials, taken through to a shaped, testable component
Permanent magnets

Net-shape Mn-Al-C magnets, Electron Beam Melting

Rare-earth-free Mn-Al-C built directly into net shape by electron beam melting, with processing optimized to maximize the desired ferromagnetic τ-phase — skipping the machining losses of casting a blank and cutting it down.

90% τ-phase yield after annealing
Magnetocaloric hardware

Metal-bonded heat exchangers, La(Fe,Mn,Si)₁₃Hₓ

Powder-bonded magnetocaloric material formed into real heat-exchanger geometry, using a low-melting metal binder chosen specifically to survive the large volume change the alloy undergoes across its phase transition.

1000s of magnetic field cycles targeted
Active magnetic regenerators

Polymer- and metal-bonded composite modules

Systematic tuning of particle size, binder type, binder fraction, and compaction pressure to turn a brittle, corrosion-prone magnetocaloric powder into a mechanically robust regenerator module ready for a refrigeration circuit.

4 process variables mapped
Thermoelectric modules

Electro-Sinter Forged ZrNiSn

A single rapid electrical-pulse-plus-forging step consolidates ZrNiSn powder directly into a dense thermoelectric leg — faster and less energy-intensive than conventional sintering, with a direct performance payoff.

−46% thermal conductivity vs. SPS
3D-printed magnets

Anisotropic Nd-Fe-B magnets by laser powder bed fusion

Two complementary LPBF routes for printing anisotropic bonded Nd-Fe-B magnets: one fabricates them from recycled powder — reclaimed from end-of-life hot-deformed magnets — bound in a low-CO₂-footprint polyamide 12; the other aligns the elongated magnetic particles mechanically during printing itself, with no external field needed.

0.73 mean particle alignment degree, ⟨cosθ⟩
Hot-deformed magnets

Spark plasma texturing of Nd-Fe-B

Field-assisted sintering (FAST/SPS) combined with hot deformation — spark plasma texturing — turns isotropic melt-spun Nd-Fe-B powder into a fully dense, anisotropic magnet. Fine control over heating rate and pressure during presintering and die-upsetting refines the microstructure beyond what conventional hot-pressing achieves.

2 SPT routes compared, same feedstock powder
Coercivity, no heavy rare earths

Solid-state hardening of Nd-Fe-B

In Nd₁₆Fe(bal)CoₓMoᵧCuᵤB₇ alloys, thermally controlling the transformation from a metastable Nd₂Fe₁₇Bₓ phase into the hard-magnetic Nd₂Fe₁₄B phase drives coercivity up sharply — reached through Co, Mo, and Cu alloying and heat treatment alone, with no dysprosium or terbium needed.

200 → 700 kA·m⁻¹ coercivity, via heat treatment
04

Process capability index

The processing routes behind the work above — from raw powder to finished component.

15 ROUTES LOGGED
P-01 · FEEDSTOCK

Arc melting

Melts high-purity alloy buttons under inert atmosphere using a concentrated electric arc — the standard first step for exploratory compositions.

P-02 · FEEDSTOCK

Induction melting

Melts larger charges rapidly and uniformly via electromagnetic induction, without direct electrode contact.

P-03 · FEEDSTOCK

Gas atomization

Breaks a molten metal stream into fine spherical powder using high-pressure gas jets — the standard feedstock for powder metallurgy and printing.

P-04 · FEEDSTOCK

Strip casting

Quenches molten alloy onto a rotating cooled wheel into thin strip, refining grain structure straight out of the melt.

P-05 · FEEDSTOCK

Melt spinning

Ejects molten alloy onto a fast-spinning cooled wheel, quenching it into ribbons with nanocrystalline or near-amorphous structure.

P-06 · POWDER PREP

Ball milling

Repeated high-energy impact refines particle size and blends powder constituents mechanically.

P-07 · POWDER PREP

Mechanical alloying

Extended milling that forces atomic-level mixing between dissimilar elements, forming new alloys directly from powder.

P-08 · POWDER PREP

Hydrogen decrepitation

Hydrogen absorption expands the lattice and fractures brittle ingots into fine powder — no mechanical milling required.

P-09 · POWDER PREP

Gas–solid reaction

Reacts a solid material with a gas atmosphere — hydrogen, nitrogen — to modify composition or magnetic properties directly.

P-10 · CONSOLIDATION

Powder metallurgy

Presses and sinters powder into near-net-shape parts, avoiding the porosity and waste of casting.

P-11 · CONSOLIDATION

Hot compaction

Consolidates powder under heat and pressure into a dense solid with a locked-in, controlled microstructure.

P-12 · CONSOLIDATION

FAST / SPS

Field-Assisted / Spark Plasma Sintering pulses current through powder under pressure, reaching full density in minutes.

P-13 · CONSOLIDATION

Electro-Sinter Forging

Combines a high-current electrical pulse with mechanical forging to consolidate powder to a dense part in a single, rapid step.

P-14 · SHAPING

Hot deformation

Forges, extrudes, or rolls heated alloy — refining grain structure while forming the final geometry.

P-15 · NET-SHAPE

Additive manufacturing

Builds parts layer by layer directly from metal powder, enabling geometries — internal channels, lattices — impossible to cast.
