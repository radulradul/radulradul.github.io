01

A career built on functional oxides and alloys

My research extends beyond magnetocalorics and magnetics to cover other functional materials for energy applications. Across more than two decades, that's meant working on a genuinely diverse set of materials systems — from multiferroic oxides to thermoelectrics — each contributing to both the fundamental physics and the technological readiness of the field.

What connects them isn't a single material class but a single question asked repeatedly in different contexts: how do you get a material's internal order — magnetic, electric, vibrational — to do useful work, reliably, at the temperatures and timescales a real device demands?

02

Multiferroics: coupling magnetism to polarization

ReMn₂O₅ (Re = Ho, Tb) · orthorhombic manganites

My early research focused on multiferroic materials — systems where magnetic and electric order parameters are coupled, so that one can, in principle, be controlled through the other.

I investigated magnetostriction in orthorhombic manganites ReMn₂O₅, where inherent magnetic frustration — competing magnetic interactions that can't all be simultaneously satisfied — is lifted by small lattice distortions. That relief of frustration gives rise to canted antiferroelectric phases: the electric dipoles tilt away from perfect antiparallel alignment rather than cancelling out cleanly.

In both the holmium and terbium compounds, I observed a colossal magnetostriction effect — a change in the material's physical dimensions in response to a magnetic field, far larger than in ordinary magnetic materials — and constructed novel phase transition diagrams that mapped the correlation between magnetostriction and polarization directly.

§

Why couple magnetism and polarization at all?

A material where an electric field can flip a magnetic state, or vice versa, opens the door to memory and sensor technologies that switch electrically but store magnetically — lower power, no moving parts. Understanding exactly how frustration, lattice distortion, and polarization interact in real oxides like ReMn₂O₅ is the groundwork that any such device eventually rests on.

03

Thermoelectrics: turning waste heat into electricity

SnSe stability · ZrNiSn via Electro-Sinter Forging

Thermoelectric materials convert heat directly into electricity — and waste heat is everywhere: engines, industrial processes, even electronics running warm. Harvesting even a fraction of it is a real lever on overall energy efficiency.

The physics that makes a good thermoelectric is almost a contradiction: you want a material that conducts electricity well but conducts heat poorly, so a temperature difference across it persists long enough to do work instead of equalizing instantly. Strong anharmonicity in the crystal lattice — atoms vibrating in ways that don't scale linearly, scattering each other's vibrations (phonons) — is one of the most reliable ways to suppress thermal conductivity without sacrificing electrical performance.

My work on SnSe-based thermoelectric compounds tackles the practical side of that promise: SnSe is prized for its intrinsically ultralow thermal conductivity, but its long-term stability under operating conditions has remained a genuine open problem. My research on the stability of SnSe-based compounds addresses that gap directly — the difference between a material that performs well in a single lab measurement and one that survives in a device.

A thermoelectric only pays for itself if it still works after ten thousand thermal cycles — stability isn't a footnote, it's the whole engineering problem.

Alongside stability, I've explored how a material gets made in the first place. Electro-Sinter Forging is a novel processing route for producing ZrNiSn thermoelectric alloys — faster and more energy-efficient than conventional routes, which matters because thermoelectric device fabrication shouldn't cost more energy than the device ever recovers.

What is the figure of merit, ZT?

ZT combines electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and the Seebeck coefficient (how much voltage a temperature difference generates) into one number. Higher ZT means more of a temperature difference converts to usable electricity instead of just leaking through as heat — it's the single figure engineers use to compare thermoelectrics across very different material families.

04

EuTiO₃: when scattering becomes the story

My work on EuTiO₃ looks at its anomalous thermal conductivity, induced by resonant scattering — a case where the transport physics itself, not just the number it produces, is the interesting result.

Materials with complex magnetic and structural phase transitions, like EuTiO₃, often develop exotic thermal and transport properties right around those transitions: local vibrational or magnetic modes can resonate with lattice phonons and scatter them selectively, producing thermal conductivity behavior that a simple model wouldn't predict. That's scientifically fascinating in its own right, and technologically promising — resonant scattering is a mechanism that, once understood, can potentially be engineered into other materials on purpose to suppress thermal conductivity where it isn't wanted, or preserve it where it is.

Probing this in EuTiO₃ feeds into the broader field of thermal management in energy materials and devices — the same underlying physics that shapes a thermoelectric's performance also governs heat flow in packaging, sensors, and power electronics.

05

Why this matters for the supply chain, too

Functional materials research has a role to play in critical-material dependence that goes beyond permanent magnets.

Thermoelectric materials based on abundant elements can enable waste-heat recovery without relying on critical raw materials — a direct alternative pathway to energy efficiency that doesn't compete for the same constrained resources as rare-earth magnets or battery chemistries. Similarly, materials engineered for enhanced thermal management can improve the efficiency and reliability of electronic devices, reducing the overall materials demand for power electronics and cooling systems downstream.

In that sense, this strand of research is less a departure from the magnetics and magnetocalorics work than a different angle on the same problem: build materials that do more, with less that's scarce.
